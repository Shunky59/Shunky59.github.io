MEDS: Autonomous Delivery Robot

Senior Capstone Design: A fully autonomous system for hospital medication delivery.

Systems Engineering Python & Flask Mechatronics

The Challenge

For our Senior Design Capstone, our team designed, built, and validated the Medical Express Delivery System (MEDS). The objective was to create an autonomous robot (1 ft √ó 1 ft, 17 lbs) capable of navigating a hospital environment, detecting "patient" distress signals via wearable sensors, and dispensing medication.

The complete system: Autonomous Robot, Koozie Docking Station, and Patient Wearables.

Our final system featured a skid-steering drivetrain for zero-radius turns, a custom rack-and-pinion dispenser, and a sophisticated fever-detection algorithm. We completed the build for $836.14, staying well under our $1,000 budget.

Electrical & Systems Architecture

I led the electrical integration for the system. The robot features a Raspberry Pi 4 acting as the central brain, communicating with a RoboClaw motor controller and an Arduino Nano for the delivery mechanism. The wearable utilizes an ESP32 for WiFi communication, enabling patients to trigger the robot via a button press or automatic fever detection.

Mechanical Integration

The robot utilized traction wheels in the back and omni wheels in the front, allowing for precise skid steering. For the medication hand-off, we designed a custom rack-and-pinion mechanism that extended the medication cup to the "Koozie" docking station, ensuring precise delivery without spillage.

Software Architecture: "The Brain"

This was the most complex technical challenge. I developed the control software using Python on the Raspberry Pi. The system utilized multi-threading to handle competing tasks: one thread managed the Flask web server for WiFi communication with patient wearables, while another thread handled sensor polling and navigation logic.

Prototyping & Iteration

We followed a rigorous design-build-test cycle. Early prototypes focused on validating the ESP32 WiFi communication range and the torque requirements for the drivetrain. Starting prototyping early with low-cost materials (cardboard and basic 3D prints) allowed us to catch integration errors weeks before the final deadline.

Project Management & Key Takeaways

Our success was built on clear communication and parallel workflow. By setting clear expectations for each subsystem owner, we avoided the "integration hell" common in capstone projects.

  • Internal Deadlines: Kept the group ahead of schedule, allowing for extensive testing time.
  • Trade Studies: Mathematical modeling (MATLAB) guided our motor selection, preventing costly hardware pivots later.
  • Simplicity Wins: We prioritized robust, simple solutions over complex ones, resulting in a 4.7/5 manufacturing quality score.
